-A brief overview of what Morse code is

1.Morse code is a method of transmitting text, numbers, and punctuation by representing them as standardized sequences of short signals ("dots" or "dits") and long signals ("dashes" or "dahs"). Developed in the 1830s-1840s by Samuel Morse and Alfred Vail for electrical telegraphs, it allowed messages to be sent instantly over long distances.

-The historical context of why Morse code was created

1.The Communication Crisis: Before the 1830s, long-distance communication was limited to physical travel (horse-and-carriage), which could take days or weeks. Messages could only travel as fast as the fastest horse.
2.Samuel Morse's Personal Tragedy: In 1825, artist Samuel Morse was away painting in Washington, D.C., when his wife became gravely ill and died in Connecticut. Due to slow communication, he only learned of her illness, death, and burial after the events, prompting his vow to find a faster method of communication
3.Invention of the Telegraph: In the 1830s, inventors were exploring electric signals to transmit information, with early systems using complicated needle mechanisms to point to letters.
4.Need for Efficiency (Dots & Dashes): Alfred Vail, working with Morse, developed the system of dots and dashes to be more efficient than early numeric codes, designing it to be easily interpreted from electrical pulses, even by ear.
5.First Official Demonstration: On May 24, 1844, Morse transmitted the first message, "What hath God wrought," from Washington, D.C., to Baltimore, marking the beginning of instant, long-distance communication.
